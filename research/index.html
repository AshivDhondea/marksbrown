Radiation Detection

Detection of ionising radiation is typically accomplished by transducing the incoming particle into light. Within a scintillator crystal this is achieved via radioluminescence, producing an isotropic burst of low-energy photons. A portion of this light will travel through the scintillator crystal and subsequently be analysed as an electrical signal produced by the photodetector; Typically a Silicon photomultiplier tube (SiPM) in modern designs.

The scintillator is comprised of three main components. A scintillator crystal, to convert incident radiation into an isotropic burst of optical photons; a photodetector to convert the generated light into an electrical signal and optical grease to improve coupling between the scintillator crystal and the photodetector. The length of the scintillator crystal L and the depth of interaction are shown.

Positron-Emission Tomography

Scintillator detectors are mainly used in medical imaging and high energy physics due to the superior energy, time and spatial resolutions they afford. In Positron-Emission Tomography (PET), thousands of scintillator detectors are arranged in rings about the patient to record 0.511MeV γγ correlation pairs generated by absorbed biologically-active radioisotope. For instance in oncology 18 F-FDG can be used to locate tumours. Detection of in excess of 100,000 true γγ pairs about a patient will allow reconstruction of the radioisotope density within the body and thus produce an image. There are many sources of noise in this tomographically produced picture that need to be overcome in the next generation of PET scanner to produce clearer images for medical practitioners[1].

One of the main sources of noise is the finite uncertainty in the γ detection time; the standard deviation of which we call the time resolution. There are two potential gains by improving the time resolution. Firstly, a reduction would lead to fewer random events being recorded as pairs and thus an overall improvement in the signal-to-noise ratio[2]. Secondly for sufficiently improved time resolution, the relative arrival time at opposing scintillator detectors could be determined with enough accuracy to place the emission region within the patient. This time-of-flight (TOF) information would lead to significant improvements in off-axis spatial resolution, dramatic cuts to scan times and increased contrast in generated tomographic images.

Scintillator crystals used in PET have a small cross section, to improve spatial resolution and are long to increase the portion of incident radiation detected. Furthermore each crystal is wrapped in a diffusive material to increase the fraction of generated isotropic light reaching the photodetector. Whilst both the geometry and wrapping are necessary for improving the PET scanner overall, both contribute to the degradation of the time resolution. In the former case, by increasing the number of interactions with surface faces and increasing the chance of absorption or escape. In the latter case by changing the deterministic nature of specular reflection to the more statistical of Lambertian reflection. In both, some information about the original pulse is lost and thus the time resolution is degraded.

A scintillator detector is comprised of a scintillator crystal coupled to a photodetector via a layer of optical grease. This light is shown interacting with a proposed scattering structure to reduce the travel time variance to the photodetector and thus improve the time resolution.

Proposed Solution

In the above image a schematic of the scintillator detector as described in the previous section can be seen. In this we see one proposed solution to reduce the time resolution whilst maintaining the advantages gained by geometry and wrapping. A side face of the scintillator crystal is coated in a periodic array of nanometre-sized plasmonic structures. By control of the shape, size and relative location of each structure within the ‘reflection metasurface’, the relative phase introduced by induced dipoles to incident light can be controlled. In doing so the outgoing direction of reflected light can be manipulated. We wish to scatter a portion of the radiation-generated isotropic light toward the photodetector. Usage of such reflection metasurfaces in scintillator detectors, to reduce the photon travel time variance, are ideally suited to this purpose, due to their low losses and their broadband nature[3].

The primary advantage to PET of improved time resolution is a much greater proportion of false events can be excluded, resulting in a significantly higher signal to noise ratio. An improved SNR will result in PET images with higher contrast, thus giving clinicians more information about their patient[4].
