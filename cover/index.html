Cover Letter

Dear Dr Hetherington or to whom it may concern,

I am apply for the position of ‘Research Software Developer’ offered on the 3rd of January seen on the Research Software Development blog. I believe I am an excellent candidate for the position and I will endeavour to prove it in this brief letter.

To begin, I code near-daily in Python for scientific applications and have done so for over 4 years. I am highly proficient with Pandas, NumPy, SciPy, Matplotlib and the uncertainties package. All reused code is written and tidied into modules subscribing to the DRY principle and the pep8 style guide for ease of collaboration with other PhD students. Furthermore I am planning to attend SciPy 2014 and EuroSciPy 2014. Also in Python, my own website uses Pelican, a static webpage generator, with a customised bootstrap3 theme. Most modules I have written, have been, or will be made, freely available on GitHub. Two of the most recent packages released so far are :

  1. pylumerical is a Python wrapper about the commercial FDTD electromagnetic simulator from Lumerical. The tool itself is excellent, however the scripting interface is poor. The wrapper has been designed to implement optimisations and parameter sweeps using custom metrics in an efficient manner. Furthermore usage of pylumerical maximises the Ã¨ngine licence usage compared to the common bottleneck of the GUI licence maximising the number of potential users. Recently the MPI layer has been altered to allow running the program remotely from within the groups’ available computing recourses.
  2. photontracing is a Monte-Carlo photon tracer with customised surface reflection models. For speed of operation, photons are grouped by face allowing us to leverage the speed of NumPy array operations to update directions efficiently. Whilst this tool was predominantly written in my spare time as a learning exercise, it’s results have been validated against analytical solutions as well my main photon tracer - Geant4. This is a C++ Monte-Carlo physics framework, primarily used for particle and medical physics.

Other technical skills that I have developed during my university education include 3D modelling and desktop publishing. In the former, an old hobby to create non-maniform 3D meshes for computer games using Blender, has become an essential skill for 3D printing. This has been most recently used in creation of a custom photodetector clamp used in radiation experiments at CERN. A picture of which can be seen in my most recent publication in figure 2. The journal article, to be submitted by February 2014, has been written using an online collaboration tool called Authorea with my colleagues at CERN. In the latter I’ve LaTeX regularly since undergraduate. I also primarily use Inkscape to generate vector graphics for presentations and papers. Examples of both can be found on this website and in the paper given above. Recently I’ve began looking at most exciting options available for conveying information including Reveal.js presentations and the Python package Bokeh.

Further to all of the the above, I’m constantly learning. My PhD is based within the interdisplinary Photonic Systems Development CDT. My PhD itself brings together different fields to create an improved system. In this case we combine a reflection metasurface, a designed nanoscaled metallic surface, with that of a scintillator detector to improve the timing performance. Positron Emission Tomography (PET) will benefit from this by production of clearer images for medical practioners.

Further to my own studies I wish to keep improving and excelling in my coding. I want to explore the potential benefits cython and pypy can bring to my work, in addition to those already brought by NumPy. I want the science I produce to be transparent and reproducible. I believe releasing the pipeline used to reach new and exciting results is essential for speeding up scientific progress. For my part, I have enjoyed the challenges offered in programming that my studies have afforded me so far. To me, research software development is a way of making academia more productive and more transparent by creation and modification of better tools. I look forward to contributing in this incredibly useful field for years to come.
